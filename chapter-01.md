# Chapter 1: Ashes of the Earth

Earth hung in the void like a tarnished marble: oceans gone the color of old copper, continents scarred by storms that came too fast to name. The atmosphere shimmered with haze and wildfire smoke, wavering like the last breath of a world that had already decided to die.

Against this backdrop, the Orion blazed in sunlight. Its massive habitat rings and solar arrays unfurled like a mechanical flower in full bloom, the product of three years of frantic construction culminating in humanity's greatest engineering achievement and its most exclusive escape plan. Fifty thousand berths. The cheapest starting in the hundreds of millions.

A countdown pulsed across every screen on the planet:

*ASCENT SHUTTLE LAUNCH — T-MINUS 6 HOURS*

Six hours until the last transport shuttles ferried the final passengers upward. Six hours until Adam Hale either saved himself or damned himself, depending on who you asked.

---

His salvaged hover-bike rattled beneath him as he threaded through the collapsed section of Highway 12, reading the broken road by instinct. Twenty-six years old, lean from years of mechanical work rather than the gene therapies the wealthy used to sculpt their children before birth. Angular face, eyes the color of faded denim that absorbed everything while giving nothing back.

He cut around a concrete barricade and past a stalled convoy of refugee transports, their passengers staring out through scratched windows with the hollow patience of people who had nowhere left to go. A patrol drone sliced overhead, red sensor locking on. His wrist monitor shrieked: citizenship verified, debt forgiven under Orion service contract. The usual digital leash. Adam gunned the engine through a flooded underpass.

He swerved around a procession of children carrying empty water containers along the highway shoulder. One of the smaller kids dropped her container and watched it roll toward the flooded gutter. Adam braked, leaned down to grab it, and held it out until she took it.

The kid didn't wave. Didn't smile. Just clutched the container to her chest and kept walking.

Five hours and forty-two minutes until the last shuttle boarded.

Five hours and forty-one minutes until he abandoned his mother to die alone.

---

The apartment building had evolved from middle-class housing into a vertical refugee camp, the kind of place where you learned to breathe through your mouth and keep your eyes on your own door. Hallways reeked of sewage backup and cooking fires. The elevator had died months ago. An AI terminal at the entrance scanned every face that passed, its screen cycling through resident profiles with the indifferent thoroughness of a predator that wasn't hungry yet.

Adam took the stairs three at a time, legs burning from the bike ride and the sprint and the particular exhaustion of a man running toward a goodbye he didn't want to reach. He passed the tenth floor (once home to his legendary parties, the abandoned community room transformed every Friday into a pulsing dreamworld of light and bass). That room was a lending terminal now. Through reinforced glass, he could see desperate people hunched before featureless screens, tears streaking their faces as red DENIED notices blazed across loan applications.

He turned away and kept climbing.

The door to 12B had three different locks: mechanical, electronic, and a deadbolt he'd fabricated from salvaged steel. He disarmed them by feel, slipped inside, sealed them again.

"The conquering hero returns," said a thin voice from the corner.

Elaine Hale lay on a hospital cot that dominated the small living space, propped up by pillows that had gone flat months ago. Adam had done what he could with the room: hand-painted constellations sprawling across the ceiling, a salvaged planetarium projector casting slow-moving stars across the walls, comic book posters curling against soot-stained concrete. A room trying very hard to be somewhere else.

"Hey, Mom." The edge in his voice vanished, just like that. Every time. "Brought you something."

He pulled a small mechanical creation from his pack: a robot dog assembled from spare parts. It walked in a wobbly circle when wound, emitted a single high-pitched bark, and cast a soft blue glow from its one remaining eye.

He set it on the bedside table and wound it. They watched it march its small circle together.

She took it with trembling hands. She'd been beautiful once, and Adam had seen it in pre-collapse photos: the same thick dark hair he'd inherited, though hers was silver now. Her frame had withered over the past two years, cheekbones sharp beneath skin that had gone gray like the sky. Lung failure. The plague of their generation. Most people called it the Fade and accepted it as the price of breathing Earth's poisoned air, the same way they accepted everything else, because what choice did they have?

"You look exactly like you did at eight years old," she said, her smile genuine beneath the oxygen tube. "Playing astronauts in the backyard."

Then, more quietly: "You don't have to do this, Adam."

He looked at the robot dog instead of her. "Yeah. I do."

"The medication —"

"Is running out, and we both know it." He busied himself adjusting the projector, unable to look at her. The stars on the ceiling shifted, realigned.

Her pain monitor shrieked from the bedside, numbers climbing on its small screen, each spike adding charges to their medical account. Adam crossed to the wall terminal and pressed his thumb to the reader.

*Transfer complete: 120 credits. Remaining treatment days: 8. Account status: critical.*

Eight days. He'd bought his mother eight more days of breathing by signing his life to Orion's maintenance division. Seven years of service in exchange for medical credits that would run out long before the ship reached Mars. The math didn't work. It had never worked. But it was all he had, and doing something useless was better than doing nothing at all.

"You're not afraid of dying," she said. "You're afraid of staying. Of watching."

He grabbed the remote and switched on the old projector instead of answering. Saturn's rings burst across the ceiling, rotating in slow cosmic dance.

For a while, neither of them spoke. The rings turned. The robot dog completed another circuit and stopped, its mechanism spent.

She studied him for a long moment. Then she reached under her pillow and withdrew a pendant on a tarnished chain, a simple metal disk with a constellation etched on one side. Orion, the hunter.

"Your father wanted you to have this when you were old enough."

Adam's fingers closed around the cool metal. His father: steelworker, then nothing, then gone. Adam had been seventeen.

"He wore it during his military service," she said. "Before everything crumbled."

Adam looped the chain around his neck. The pendant settled against his sternum, cold at first, then warming.

"That ship is built on bones, Adam."

"And I'm built on bad jokes. We all have our foundations."

She watched him with eyes too wise for his comfort. He busied himself winding the little robot dog again, watching it march its pointless circle, bark its pointless bark.

Later, while she slept (or pretended to), he recorded a message on her tablet. He talked about Mars. About what he'd build there. Somewhere in the middle, his voice broke, and he sat with the silence for a long time before finishing.

He deleted the recording. Started again, steadier, and scheduled it to play after the shuttle launched.

He slid the family photo off the wall and into his pocket. In it, they stood beside a lake, smiling with the easy confidence of people who believed in tomorrow. Three faces. One dead. One dying. One leaving.

As he turned to go, he caught her reflection in the projector glass. Her eyes were open. She'd watched him take the photo.

Neither said a word about it.

"I'll send you a postcard from Mars," he said.

"Make it a good one," she replied.

The last lie they would tell each other.

---

He nearly missed the transport.

His hover-bike's stabilizer failed in a metro tunnel. Deliberate obsolescence, technology engineered to break when replacement parts would generate maximum profit. The vehicle ground against the concrete wall until he wrestled it to a stop. Blood from a cut above his eye. Three hundred meters to the checkpoint.

The security officer at the ramp consulted his tablet with the unhurried indifference of a man whose own seat was guaranteed. "Boarding closed at 4:25."

"System said 4:30."

Adam rattled off maintenance codes and protocol numbers until the officer's certainty wavered. In that hesitation, he was through the door and into the nearest seat as the ramp sealed behind him.

His wrist monitor flashed: *PENALTY FOR LATE ARRIVAL: 120 CREDITS. ACCOUNT STATUS: NEGATIVE.*

One more debt on his cosmic tab. But he was moving.

---

The transport crawled along the broken highway, and Adam watched the world die through a scratched window.

Flooded fields stretched to the horizon. A megachurch converted to an emergency shelter. Tent cities along rivers that ran the wrong color. At every overpass, military checkpoints: soldiers in environmental suits, automated turrets tracking the road.

The systems engineer beside him gripped a tablet with white knuckles, monitor bracelet glowing blue with educational debt. Adam offered him a piece of contraband gum. They rode in silence while the world scrolled past.

In a passing convoy, a boy pressed his face against a bus window, tears cutting channels through the dirt on his cheeks. A small electronic badge on his shirt: DEPENDENT: AWAITING PROCESSING.

Adam looked away first. A small, ugly relief — *I'm on this side of the glass* — that he couldn't quite smother. His father would have stopped the transport. His father would have demanded they open the doors.

His father was dead, and Adam was leaving, and the difference between those two facts was the whole of his inheritance.

---

Launch Site 7 was a cathedral of metal and concrete, and outside its electrified perimeter, tens of thousands of people had come to watch the end of the world.

They pressed against the fences: a sea of faces and raised signs. TAKE US WITH YOU. OUR CHILDREN DESERVE STARS. And, on a piece of cardboard held by an old woman standing very still: PLEASE. Just that. Nothing else needed.

Inside the perimeter, a different reality. Pristine walkways. Refreshment tables offering things Adam hadn't seen in years: actual fruit, drinks served in real glass. He watched from the maintenance section (hard benches, no refreshments) while General Morea's broadcast played on every surface, the usual speech about pioneers and new worlds.

Outside the fence, sonic deterrents fired against a crowd surge. Inside, attendants poured champagne into crystal.

Adam broke a hoarded chocolate bar into pieces and passed them silently to the workers around him.

---

The shuttle's interior made the hierarchy literal. Forward cabin: passengers in tailored uniforms sipping from actual glass. Rear cabin: narrow seats, overhead screens displaying each passenger's debt obligations and contract terms, air that tasted recycled before the engines even started.

Adam stowed his things beneath the seat: cracked comic book, family photo, pendant under his shirt, beatmaker and luminescent headphones. Everything he owned in the world, which was also everything he owned between worlds.

Near the front, a small commotion. An older man in a simple but expensive suit arguing with a flight attendant, gesturing toward the rear cabin. Behind him, Artemis Morea, looking uncomfortable in a way that had nothing to do with the shuttle.

Adam caught fragments: "— perfectly capable of riding with the maintenance crew. These ridiculous distinctions —"

Her voice, quieter: "Father will notice your absence."

They passed down the aisle and took seats several rows ahead. A botanist who'd chosen the cheap seats on principle, and the general's daughter who'd followed him there. Adam filed it away.

The engines roared. The shuttle punched through the atmosphere with a sound like the world tearing open. Gravity became a living weight, pressing him into the seat, turning every breath into a negotiation. Someone behind him was praying.

Then — silence. Weightlessness.

Through the window, Earth shrank. The ash-gray sky gave way to absolute black, and the planet that had birthed and failed them pulled away, a sick marble spinning in emptiness, the devastation that had driven them here rendered beautiful and abstract by distance.

And above them, growing from a point of light into a sprawling reality: the Orion. Habitat rings rotating with mechanical constancy. Solar arrays catching unfiltered sun. A city in the void, waiting.

Adam pressed his forehead against the cold window. His reflection overlaid the view: tired eyes, unruly hair, a face that belonged in neither world.

He let his face go honest. Just a young man watching everything he'd known become a point of light, leaving behind a mother who had eight days of breathing left and a planet that had even less.

He sat with it longer than he meant to. The grief, and beneath the grief, that same ugly relief, the engine that had carried him this far and would carry him further, whether he wanted it to or not.

The last image burned into his mind was his mother's eyes (open, watching) reflected in the projector glass as he walked out the door.
