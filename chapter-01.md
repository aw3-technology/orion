# Chapter 1: Ashes of the Earth

Earth hung in the void like a tarnished marble — oceans gone the color of old copper, continents scarred by storms that no longer had names because there were too many to name. The atmosphere shimmered with haze and wildfire smoke, wavering like the last breath of something that had already decided to stop breathing.

Against this dying backdrop, the Orion blazed in sunlight. Massive habitat rings and solar arrays unfurling like a mechanical flower in full bloom. Three years of frantic construction, culminating in humanity's greatest engineering achievement and its most exclusive escape plan. Ten thousand berths. Accommodation starting in the hundreds of millions.

A countdown pulsed across every screen on the planet:

*ASCENT SHUTTLE LAUNCH — T-MINUS 6 HOURS*

Six hours until the last transport shuttles ferried the final passengers upward. Six hours until Adam Hale either saved himself or damned himself, depending on who you asked.

---

His salvaged hover-bike screamed beneath him as he carved through the collapsed section of Highway 12, body melding with the machine on instinct. Twenty-six years old, lean from years of mechanical work rather than the gene therapies the wealthy used to sculpt their children before birth. Angular face, high cheekbones that gave him a perpetually hungry look — a look he'd learned to mask with an easy grin. Eyes the color of faded denim that absorbed everything while giving nothing back.

Neo-trance blasted from the bike's speakers, bass synchronizing with his heartbeat the way it always did. Since sixteen, he'd run the underground raves in the industrial district. Salvaged light arrays, speakers wired from scrap, six hours of forgetting every Friday night for anyone who could find the door. He'd been good at it — making people forget. He'd been practicing on himself even longer.

He launched the bike over a concrete barricade and sailed past a stalled convoy of refugee transports, their passengers staring out through scratched windows with the hollow patience of people who had nowhere left to go.

A patrol drone sliced overhead, red sensor locking on. The monitor clamped to his wrist shrieked — citizenship verified, balance at 247 credits, debt forgiven under Orion service contract. The usual digital leash, reminding him that even his body was collateral now.

Adam flipped the drone off and gunned the engine through a flooded underpass, water exploding behind him.

Past a government billboard cycling through propaganda — General Morea's digital face towering against the ash-gray sky, jaw set in manufactured determination. As Adam roared past, the billboard's sensors caught his wrist monitor's signal and the display flickered, personalizing itself: ALEX REEVES: YOUR SKILLS SECURED YOUR FUTURE.

"Guess I got the discount package," he muttered.

He swerved around a procession of children carrying empty water containers along the highway shoulder, supervised by a single exhausted woman in a volunteer vest. One of the smaller kids dropped her container and watched it roll toward the flooded gutter. Adam swooped down, snatched it one-handed, and tossed it back without slowing.

The kid didn't wave. Didn't smile. Just clutched the container to her chest and kept walking.

Five hours and forty-two minutes until the last shuttle boarded.

Five hours and forty-one minutes until he abandoned his mother to die alone.

---

The apartment building had evolved from middle-class housing into a vertical refugee camp, the kind of place where you learned to breathe through your mouth and keep your eyes on your own door. Hallways reeked of sewage backup and cooking fires. The elevator had died months ago. An AI terminal at the entrance scanned every face that passed, its screen cycling through resident profiles with the indifferent thoroughness of a predator that wasn't hungry yet.

Adam took the stairs three at a time, legs burning from the bike ride and the sprint and the particular exhaustion of a man running toward something he didn't want to reach. He passed the tenth floor — once home to his legendary parties, the abandoned community room transformed every Friday into a pulsing dreamworld of light and bass. That room was a lending terminal now. Through reinforced glass, he could see desperate people hunched before featureless screens, tears streaking their faces as red DENIED notices blazed across loan applications.

He turned away and kept climbing.

The door to 12B had three different locks — mechanical, electronic, and a deadbolt he'd fabricated from salvaged steel. He disarmed them by feel, slipped inside, sealed them again.

"The conquering hero returns," said a thin voice from the corner.

Elaine Hale lay on a hospital cot that dominated the small living space, propped up by pillows that had gone flat months ago. Adam had done what he could with the room — hand-painted constellations sprawling across the ceiling, a salvaged planetarium projector casting slow-moving stars across the walls, comic book posters curling against soot-stained concrete. A room trying very hard to be somewhere else.

"Hey, Mom." The edge in his voice vanished, just like that. Every time. "Brought you something."

He pulled a small mechanical creation from his pack with a magician's flourish — a robot dog assembled from spare parts. It walked in a wobbly circle when wound, emitted a single high-pitched bark, and cast a soft blue glow from its one remaining eye.

"For Martian kids who hate bedtime," he said. "The light pulses at a frequency that stimulates melatonin production." He tapped his temple. "Science."

"And where did you find a melatonin modulator? Those are military-grade."

"Found it."

"Found it attached to something else, I'm guessing."

"Details, details." He waved a hand and produced a ridiculous object from his backpack — a space helmet crafted from a cereal box, complete with a hand-painted Orion mission patch and a visor cut from a plastic bottle. He perched it on his head at a jaunty angle. "How do I look? Ready to conquer the cosmos?"

She took the toy with trembling hands. She'd been beautiful once — Adam had seen it in pre-collapse photos, the same thick dark hair he'd inherited, though hers was silver now. Her frame had withered over the past two years, cheekbones sharp beneath skin that had gone gray like the sky. Lung failure. The plague of their generation. Most people called it the Fade and accepted it as the price of breathing Earth's poisoned air, the same way they accepted everything else — because what choice did they have?

"You look exactly like you did at eight years old," she said, her smile genuine beneath the oxygen tube. "Playing astronauts in the backyard."

Then, more quietly: "You don't have to do this, Adam."

The cereal-box helmet suddenly felt heavy. "Yeah. I do."

"The medication —"

"Is running out, and we both know it." He busied himself adjusting the projector, unable to look at her. The stars on the ceiling shifted, realigned.

Her pain monitor shrieked from the bedside — numbers climbing on its small screen, each spike adding charges to their medical account. Adam crossed to the wall terminal and pressed his thumb to the reader.

*Transfer complete: 120 credits. Remaining treatment days: 8. Account status: critical.*

Eight days. He'd bought his mother eight more days of breathing by signing his life to Orion's maintenance division. Seven years of service in exchange for medical credits that would run out long before the ship reached Mars. The math didn't work. It had never worked. But it was all he had, and doing something useless was better than doing nothing at all.

"You're not afraid of dying," she said. She had always been able to see through him — through the jokes, the deflections, the careful performances. "You're afraid of staying. Of watching."

He grabbed the remote and switched on the old projector instead of answering. Saturn's rings burst across the ceiling, rotating in slow cosmic dance.

"Did you know the particles in Saturn's rings range from microscopic to mountain-sized?" he said, voice artificially bright. "The whole system dances between chaos and order. Garbage and beauty, all mixed together." He watched the rings turn. "Feels familiar."

She studied him for a long moment. Then she reached under her pillow and withdrew a pendant on a tarnished chain — a simple metal disk with a constellation etched on one side. Orion, the hunter.

"Your father wanted you to have this when you were old enough."

Adam's fingers closed around the cool metal. His father — steelworker, then nothing after the AI systems took every factory job that still existed, then a frozen body on a park bench with veins full of synthetic hallucinogen. Another casualty of the wave of addiction that had swept the industrial districts after the machines made human hands obsolete. Adam had identified him at the city morgue between repair shifts, seventeen years old and already fluent in the particular language of loss that doesn't translate into any other tongue.

"He wore it during his military service," she said. "Before everything crumbled."

Adam looped the chain around his neck. The pendant settled against his sternum, cold at first, then warming.

"That ship is built on bones, Adam."

"And I'm built on bad jokes. We all have our foundations."

She watched him with eyes too wise for his comfort. He busied himself winding the little robot dog again, watching it march its pointless circle, bark its pointless bark.

Later, while she slept — or pretended to — he recorded a message on her tablet. He started with jokes. Somewhere in the middle, his voice broke as memories flooded through the cracks: his father teaching him to fix an engine in the driveway, grease on both their hands. His mother bringing lemonade. The sun bright in a sky that was still blue. A lake they'd driven to on weekends, its water clean enough to swim in.

He deleted the recording. Made a new one — pure jokes, no cracks in the armor — and scheduled it to play after the shuttle launched.

He slid the family photo off the wall and into his pocket. In it, they stood beside that lake, smiling with the easy confidence of people who believed in tomorrow. Three faces. One dead. One dying. One leaving.

As he turned to go, he caught her reflection in the projector glass. Her eyes were open. She'd watched him take the photo.

Neither said a word about it.

"I'll send you a postcard from Mars," he said, the pendant warm against his chest now.

"Make it a good one," she replied.

The last lie they would tell each other.

---

He nearly missed the transport.

His hover-bike's stabilizer failed halfway through an abandoned metro tunnel — the right thruster sputtering and dying, the vehicle slamming sideways into the concrete wall in a shower of sparks. Not poor design. Deliberate obsolescence. The wealthy rode fifth-generation machines with self-repairing components. Everyone else got technology engineered to break precisely when replacement parts would generate maximum profit.

Adam ditched the bike at speed, hit the ground rolling, took the impact on his shoulder and came up running. Blood from a cut above his eye. Three hundred meters to the checkpoint. His wrist monitor screaming about transport departure.

He sprinted the distance on legs that had nothing left, lungs burning with the same poisoned air that was killing his mother. The transport's engines were already cycling up when he reached the ramp. The security officer at the bottom consulted his tablet with the unhurried indifference of a man whose own seat was guaranteed.

"Boarding closed at 4:25."

"System said 4:30."

"Policy changed this morning. Security protocol upgrade."

Missing this transport meant forfeiting his shuttle seat. Contract voided. His mother's medical credits terminated immediately. The math was simple: get on this vehicle or watch his mother's remaining eight days collapse to zero.

Adam did the only thing he could — rattled off maintenance codes and emergency protocol numbers, half of them real and half invented on the spot, delivered with enough bloody-faced conviction to create three seconds of doubt. In those three seconds, he was through the door and down the aisle, collapsing into the nearest empty seat as the ramp sealed behind him.

His wrist monitor flashed: *PENALTY FOR LATE ARRIVAL: 120 CREDITS. ACCOUNT STATUS: NEGATIVE.*

One more debt on his cosmic tab. But the pendant was still around his neck, and he was moving.

---

The transport crawled along the broken highway, and Adam watched the world die through a scratched window.

Flooded fields stretched to the horizon, the water catching the sick light of a sun filtered through permanent haze. A megachurch converted to an emergency shelter, its parking lot a grid of military tents. Tent cities sprawling along the banks of rivers that ran the wrong color. At every overpass, military checkpoints — soldiers in environmental suits, automated turrets tracking the road, signs stark against the gray: UNAUTHORIZED MOVEMENT PROHIBITED.

Inside the transport, screens cycled through news that wasn't news — satellite footage of a scorched Eastern seaboard, financial tickers showing Orion credits at all-time highs and terrestrial property values in free fall, the ship itself gleaming in orbit above it all like a jewel displayed for buyers who couldn't afford it.

The systems engineer beside him — young, probably fresh from one of the last universities still running — drummed his fingers against a tablet, his monitor bracelet glowing blue with educational debt.

"First ascent?" Adam asked.

"Is it that obvious?"

"You've got that 'about to vomit from existential crisis' look." Adam dug a piece of contraband gum from his pocket. "Helps with the nausea. Physical and philosophical."

"They said we're building the future."

"Yeah," Adam said. "They say a lot of things."

He offered the kid his gaming console — held together with electrical tape, loaded with games older than both of them — and they played in silence while the world scrolled past.

In a passing convoy, a boy pressed his face against a bus window. Tears had cut channels through the dirt on his cheeks. He wore a small electronic badge: DEPENDENT: AWAITING PROCESSING.

Something about his expression hooked into Adam's chest — a recognition older than memory. Adam at twelve, standing in the kitchen doorway, watching his father set his toolbox down for the last time. The way his shoulders had collapsed. The way the house had gone quiet.

*Smile, even when you're burning, son.*

Adam's fingers found the pendant. He turned it between thumb and forefinger and went back to the game.

---

Launch Site 7 was a cathedral of metal and concrete, and outside its electrified perimeter, tens of thousands of people had come to watch the end of the world.

They pressed against the fences — a sea of faces and raised signs. TAKE US WITH YOU. OUR CHILDREN DESERVE STARS. And, on a piece of cardboard held by an old woman standing very still: PLEASE. Just that. Nothing else needed.

Inside the perimeter, a different reality. Pristine walkways. Hidden speakers playing soft music. On massive screens, the Orion rotated in orbit, sunlight gliding across its hull. Refreshment tables offered things Adam hadn't seen in years — actual fruit, molecular gastronomy creations in colors that didn't exist in nature, drinks served in real glass.

He didn't try for the appetizers this time. He'd learned to read the invisible lines — which spaces tolerated his presence and which ones would flag him for simply existing in the wrong square meter.

General Morea's broadcast played on every surface. Adam watched from the maintenance section — hard benches, no refreshments — as the hologram materialized. Morea's face mapped old campaigns, lines carved deep around his mouth, hair gone steel-gray but still cut to military precision. His voice filled the space without effort.

"We gather today at the edge of history. Not as refugees from a dying world, but as pioneers of a new one."

The words followed a template Adam recognized — persuasive rhythms designed to inspire while dampening the urge to ask questions. He'd heard a hundred speeches built on the same architecture. But the crowd leaned in. They always did.

Morea introduced the dignitaries. Prince Virel of the Rising Sun Order — the Eastern coalition's envoy — moved with a dancer's grace and watched with a predator's eyes. His charisma felt engineered, every gesture landing exactly where it was meant to land. "East and West, united in humanity's greatest venture," he said, and Adam noticed how Morea's jaw tightened at the words.

Then the image shifted. A young woman appeared beside the General — dark hair pulled back simply, wearing muted blue instead of military green.

"My daughter, Artemis Morea. Cultural attaché and mission historian."

She offered no speech. No practiced words. Just a measured nod, and eyes that seemed to look through the camera at something only she could see.

The broadcast editors probably hadn't noticed what Adam noticed — her gaze drifting to a checkpoint in the background where a young maintenance worker had collapsed. Security dragged the woman upright with casual roughness. For one instant, Artemis's composure cracked. Her fingers twitched forward, the beginning of a gesture that might have been protest or intervention. Then she caught herself, and the mask smoothed back into place.

Outside the perimeter fence, sonic deterrents fired against a crowd surge. Inside, attendants poured champagne into crystal.

Adam broke a hoarded chocolate bar into pieces and passed them silently to the workers around him.

---

The shuttle's interior made the hierarchy literal. Forward cabin: passengers in tailored uniforms sipping from actual glass, attended by staff trained in the particular art of making the wealthy feel inevitable. Rear cabin: narrow seats, overhead screens displaying each passenger's debt obligations and contract terms, air that tasted recycled before the engines even started.

Adam's seat pinched. He stowed his things beneath it — the cracked comic book, family photo in his pocket, pendant under his shirt, beatmaker and luminescent headphones. Everything he owned in the world, which was also everything he owned between worlds.

Torres, the water reclamation specialist beside him, gripped the armrests with white knuckles. "They say the G-forces can make you black out."

"Lucky for you, I skipped breakfast," Adam said. "Nothing to worry about on this end."

Near the shuttle's front, a small commotion. Adam watched an older man in a simple but expensive suit arguing with a flight attendant, gesturing toward the rear cabin. Behind him, Artemis Morea, looking uncomfortable in a way that had nothing to do with the shuttle and everything to do with the argument.

Adam caught fragments: "— perfectly capable of riding with the maintenance crew. These ridiculous distinctions —"

Her voice, quieter: "Father will notice your absence."

"Let him. Someone needs to start —"

They passed down the aisle and took seats several rows ahead. A botanist who'd chosen the cheap seats on principle, and the general's daughter who'd followed him there. Adam filed it away.

The engines roared. The shuttle shook. Adam slipped on his headphones, found the track with the bass that matched his heartbeat, and closed his eyes.

Gravity became a living weight, pressing him into the seat, compressing his ribs, turning every breath into a negotiation. The shuttle punched through the atmosphere with a sound like the world tearing open. Torres made a noise beside him. Someone behind them was praying.

Then — silence. Weightlessness. The crushing force gone, replaced by a lightness that felt like falling upward. Adam's stomach lurched as his inner ear fought to recalibrate.

Through the window, Earth shrank. The ash-gray sky gave way to absolute black, and the planet that had birthed and failed them pulled away — a sick marble spinning in emptiness, storm systems visible across its face, the devastation that had driven them here rendered beautiful and abstract by distance.

Torres whispered something that might have been a prayer or a curse.

And above them, growing from a point of light into a sprawling reality: the Orion. Habitat rings rotating with mechanical precision. Solar arrays catching unfiltered sun. A city in the void, waiting.

Adam pressed his forehead against the cold window. His reflection overlaid the view — tired eyes, unruly hair, a face that belonged in neither world. Behind the reflection, Earth kept shrinking.

He let his face go honest. No grin. No mask. No performance. Just a young man watching everything he'd known become a point of light, holding his dead father's pendant so tightly the metal edges cut into his palm, leaving behind a mother who had eight days of breathing left and a planet that had even less.

He held it for ten seconds. Then he wiped his eyes, cracked his neck, and turned to Torres with the easy grin back in place.

"Ready for the greatest adventure of our lives?"

Torres managed a shaky smile. "Ready as I'll ever be."

Adam winked and said something about space toilets. Torres laughed. The tension broke, just a little.

No one needed to know what was underneath. No one needed to see the marks the pendant had left on his palm, or know that he could still hear the crowd outside the fence, or that the last image burned into his mind was his mother's eyes — open, watching — reflected in the projector glass as he walked out the door.

Time to play the role of a lifetime: the boy who didn't care that the world was ending.
