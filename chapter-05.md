# Chapter 5: Fire in the Circuit

The first week on Environmental Systems Team Alpha taught Adam that promotion was just surveillance with better lighting.

His new station sat at the junction of three atmospheric regulation nodes, a glass-walled booth on C-Deck where real-time environmental data scrolled across four separate displays. Temperature gradients, humidity curves, CO₂ ratios, pressure differentials for every habitable section of the ship. It was a significant step up from waste reclamation: cleaner air, wider corridors, a meal allowance that included actual protein once a day rather than the nutrient slurry D-Deck served as sustenance. His workband now opened transit lifts to B-Deck and C-Deck without authorization delays, and his maintenance credentials carried a clearance suffix that made security officers glance at his uniform instead of through him.

But the booth had a window that faced the main C-Deck concourse, and Supervisor Kwan's office sat thirty meters away with a direct sightline. Adam's shift reports now routed to two oversight committees instead of one. His atmospheric readings were cross-referenced against OmniscientAI baselines every four hours. And his workband logged location data at intervals he was fairly sure hadn't been in the original employment contract.

The leash was longer. It was still a leash.

He'd mentioned as much to Zedd during a late-night session in Unit 12-17, the two of them hunched over Adam's beatmaker with a blanket draped over the speaker to muffle the sound.

"They promote you to watch you better," Zedd said, adjusting a low-frequency filter with the concentration of a man defusing ordnance. "Standard playbook. More access means more exposure. More exposure means more data points for whoever's tracking you."

"You're tracking me too, technically."

"Yeah, but I like your music." Zedd glanced up, dead serious beneath the joke. "The admin log ping I told you about: it's gone quiet. Could mean they lost interest. Could mean they're watching from further back." He handed the beatmaker back. "Either way: boring shifts, clean reports, no improvisation above your clearance. For now."

Adam had followed the advice. Seven days of exemplary routine: calibrating sensors, filing reports, eating protein in the C-Deck canteen where the lighting was softer and the conversations more careful. He smiled at colleagues, answered questions about his work history with the practiced vagueness of someone used to editing his own biography. At night he lay in his bunk and listened to the ship breathe, the deep, tidal rhythm of ten thousand lives suspended in metal and vacuum, and tried not to think about the tapping through the walls that had answered his music and then gone silent.

He tried not to think about who had been on the other end.

---

The Orion's artificial evening had settled across all decks, lighting dimmed to the soft amber glow designed to maintain circadian rhythms. In the corridors of A-Deck, where the mission's highest-ranking members resided, the engineered tranquility was rarely disturbed by mechanical failure.

Which made the sudden, insistent alarm in Artemis Morea's private quarters all the more unusual.

Oxygen filtration node 47-A: flow rate fluctuation beyond acceptable parameters. Carbon dioxide rising. Pressure differentials destabilizing. Category 2, not immediately life-threatening but requiring prompt attention. A repair technician was dispatched with an elite-level override.

Artemis sat at her meditation altar, breathing deliberately as the warning tones pulsed. The system had sealed her quarters and activated secondary oxygen supply, though the failure wasn't yet critical.

When the door chimed, she answered with practiced composure.

"Access granted."

---

Adam Hale had been in the middle of recalibrating a waste recycling junction in the ship's lower decks when the elite-level override alert interrupted his assigned task list. He stared at the notification on his workband with a mixture of annoyance and resignation.

Priority maintenance request: Environmental system malfunction in A-Deck residential quarters, Section 3, Unit A-7.

A-Deck. Of course. Heaven forbid some elite passenger experience a two-degree temperature fluctuation in their climate-controlled paradise while half the crew quarters on D-Deck struggled with humidity regulation issues he'd been reporting for weeks without response.

"You're being summoned to the promised land," Huang Wei observed from his position at a nearby control panel, noticing Adam's expression. "Some diplomat's shower not producing the perfect water pressure?"

"Environmental control issue in a premium suite," Adam replied, gathering his tools. "Probably just someone adjusting settings beyond operating parameters and wondering why the system objected."

"Better you than me," Huang sympathized. "I always say the wrong thing to those people."

As Adam secured his equipment, another figure appeared in the maintenance corridor: Zedd, hands deep in a deactivated air filter housing, grease smeared across his knuckles.

"Hey, before you go fixing paradise," Zedd said casually, without looking up, "you should know, your last access trace pinged something in the admin logs."

Adam paused. "You sure?"

"Positive. Looks like someone upstream took notice. Just… stick to the script up there, yeah? No side installs, no clever reroutes."

Adam gave him a look. "What do you think I'm gonna do—pipe jazz into their air vents?"

Zedd smirked. "I've seen your toolkit. Wouldn't put it past you."

He nodded once, then turned back to his work. "Keep it boring, Hale. Just for today."

Adam offered a short wave and headed toward the nearest transit lift, one of the few that had access codes for A-Deck. As the lift ascended through the ship's layers, passing B and C Decks on its way to the uppermost level, he arranged his expression into something neutral and forgettable. Professional. Efficient. Gone the moment the job was done.

The lift doors opened into a corridor that bore little resemblance to the utilitarian passages of the lower decks. Here, the lighting was softer, the air subtly perfumed, the walls covered in actual material rather than exposed metal and composite. Adam's orange maintenance uniform felt particularly garish against the muted elegance of his surroundings.

He followed the guidance markers on his workband to Unit A-7, noting with raised eyebrows the location designation. Not just any A-Deck residence, but one in the command section, quarters assigned to the highest tier of mission leadership.

The security panel beside the door scanned his credentials before announcing his presence to the occupant. After a brief pause, the door slid open with a nearly silent hydraulic sigh.

Adam stepped into luxury unlike anything available on the lower decks: a space designed for psychological comfort and aesthetic pleasure as much as function. The room was minimalist yet undeniably opulent, with actual physical books on shelves, hand-woven textiles from Earth's dwindling artisan communities, and a viewport that offered an unobstructed view of the stars, a privilege denied to most aboard the Orion.

In the center of this carefully curated environment sat Artemis Morea, daughter of General Christopher Morea and cultural attaché of the mission. Adam had glimpsed her during official broadcasts but never expected to encounter her directly. She was seated on a meditation platform, her back straight, her expression composed despite the environmental alert still pulsing softly throughout the quarters.

"Maintenance responding to environmental system failure," Adam announced formally, keeping his tone neutral. "The alert indicates an oxygen filtration malfunction."

"Yes," Artemis replied, her voice surprisingly direct for someone of her station. "The primary filtration node began cycling erratically approximately twelve minutes ago. Secondary systems engaged as designed, but the source malfunction continues."

Adam blinked. He had expected vague complaints or imperious demands, not a technical assessment that could have come from a systems engineer.

"I'll need to access the environmental control panel behind the eastern wall section," he said, already moving toward the indicated area, tools in hand.

"Of course," Artemis responded, rising from her meditation platform with fluid grace. "The access point is adjacent to the atmospheric regulation display."

As Adam removed the wall panel to expose the complex network of environmental control systems, he found himself watching Artemis from the corner of his eye. She observed his work with quiet interest, not impatience.

"Looks like a calibration failure in the primary oxygen sensor," he muttered, more to himself than to her. "System's reading phantom carbon dioxide spikes and overcorrecting."

"Which triggers the filtration cycle to accelerate beyond normal parameters," Artemis added, completing his analysis.

Adam turned to look at her directly, unable to mask his surprise. "You know environmental systems?"

Something flickered across her face, there and gone before he could name it. "I find ship mechanics interesting," she replied, her tone deliberately casual.

"Rich-girl panic got me up here for nothing," Adam said under his breath as he turned back to the control panel, the words escaping before he could filter them.

The silence that followed was sharp enough to cut. When Artemis finally spoke, her voice had cooled several degrees.

"I believe you'll find the malfunction is quite real, Technician Hale. Not panic, not entitlement. An actual system failure requiring actual expertise."

Adam's hands stilled on the control panel. She had noted his name without him providing it. He turned to face her again, reassessing.

"Must be tough," he said, unable to entirely suppress his ingrained resentment, "breathing rarified air while the rest of us fix the pipes."

"Must be easy," she countered without missing a beat, "pretending cynicism is clarity."

Their eyes locked.

Adam returned to the control panel, focusing on the actual repair rather than the unsettling exchange. He disassembled the sensor array to access the calibration mechanism, methodically tracing the failure path through the system's components.

To his surprise, Artemis moved closer, kneeling beside him to observe the process.

"There," she said suddenly, pointing to a minor subsystem junction he hadn't yet examined. "The auxiliary feedback loop is showing irregularities consistent with quantum interference."

Adam stared at her, genuine shock replacing his practiced indifference. "You read schematics?"

Her chin lifted a fraction. "I designed a life-support backup in my second year. Before I was assigned this... role."

She stopped there, visibly pulling back from whatever she'd been about to say next. Her expression closed like a door, not hostile, but final. Whatever else she knew about the ship's systems, she wasn't sharing it with a maintenance worker she'd met twenty minutes ago.

Adam returned to the repair, conscious now of the different weight in her attention. He found the source of the interference (a degraded isolation component allowing quantum fluctuations to affect sensor readings) and replaced it with practiced efficiency.

As he reassembled the control panel, his gaze caught on something out of place in the carefully curated environment: a worn book on her nightstand, its cover faded from handling rather than designed to appear antique. The title was just visible from his position: *Myths of the Old World*.

Adam couldn't help himself; he tilted his head slightly to read some of the chapter titles visible on the open page: Prometheus, Inanna, Moses, The Dreamtime. Not the sanitized mythology approved for Mars cultural preservation, but the original stories with all their messy contradictions and uncomfortable truths.

Artemis noticed his attention, but said nothing.

Adam closed the access panel, the repair complete. "Environmental system is stable," he reported, his voice returning to professional neutrality. "The oxygen filtration cycle should function normally now."

"Thank you, Technician Hale," Artemis replied with equal formality. She didn't look away.

Adam gathered his tools, preparing to return to the lower decks and his interrupted maintenance schedule. As he moved toward the door, it hissed open unexpectedly, revealing Prince Virel.

The diplomatic envoy of the Rising Order paused in the doorway, his gaze finding Adam before his smile did. He filled the frame the way certain men fill rooms, not with size but with the particular stillness of someone accustomed to being watched.

"Maintenance," Virel observed, his tone making the word both acknowledgment and dismissal. "I trust the issue has been resolved?"

"Yes, sir," Adam replied, his expression carefully blank. "Sensor calibration failure. Corrected now."

Virel's gaze moved between Adam and Artemis. "Your efficiency is appreciated," he said finally.

"Happy to keep the oxygen flowing for the chosen," Adam responded, the honorific just ambiguous enough to avoid clear insubordination.

Virel's eyes narrowed slightly, but his smile held. "Keep it flowing," he said, already looking past Adam to the control panel. "It's noticed when it doesn't."

Adam nodded and moved past Virel into the corridor, the door closing behind him with decisive finality. He stood for a moment in the opulent hallway, processing the unexpected encounter before heading back toward the transit lift and his proper place in the ship's order.

Inside the private quarters, Virel approached Artemis. "A system failure?" he inquired, though his tone suggested the question was merely prelude.

"A minor malfunction," Artemis confirmed, her diplomatic mask firmly back in place. "Resolved now."

"Good," Virel nodded. "I've come to discuss the cultural integration protocols for the Mars settlement. The Rising Order has some suggestions regarding optimal preservation methodologies."

Artemis responded with appropriate interest, engaging in the discussion with practiced expertise. The lights flickered once more, then stabilized. But something in her hadn't. Not since he looked at her like she was a person rather than a post.
