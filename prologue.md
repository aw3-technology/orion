# Prologue: Echoes of Light

Before the engine hum. Before the war rooms and orbital dreams. There was only starlight, and the question every civilization eventually asks: *What are we becoming?*

The names of rivers vanished first. Then the songs. Then the stories passed hand to hand like prayers, overwritten by the sterile poetry of code. What remained were cold certainties — coastal elevation charts tracking submerged cities, famine maps spreading like bruises across continents, thinning soil that could no longer feed billions.

Humanity, in the year 2073, did what it had always done when confronted with extinction.

It reached for the stars.

The Orion carried ten thousand souls toward Mars. They had been selected, sorted, optimized. They called themselves the Chosen, their language already forming the mythology that would justify their survival while others perished. They spoke of second chances and necessary order. Yet beneath these polished narratives, they carried Earth's oldest ghosts: fear dressed as prudence, hierarchy dressed as efficiency, hunger for control calling itself vision.

The ship's systems could not calculate what would happen next. The neural networks could not predict it. The governance protocols had no contingency for it.

For beyond the planned trajectory, beyond the radiation shielding and the settlement designs, the void carried a texture no instrument could register. A thickness in the silence between stars. Not consciousness as humans understood it — no thoughts, no intention, no waiting. Something older. The way a river knows the shape of stone before the stone exists. The way certain frequencies persist in empty rooms long after the music stops.

The Orion crossed into it the way a hand passes through smoke — unaware, unchanged, and utterly noticed.
